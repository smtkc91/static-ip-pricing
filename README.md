## Quick Definition: What a Static IP Actually Is

A static IP address is an IP that stays the same between sessions. It doesn't rotate, it doesn't get reassigned, it doesn't shuffle when your router reboots. Whether you're hosting a service, whitelisting a connection, scraping data, or running a dedicated proxy, the address remains bound to your account or port until you release it.

Dynamic IPs, by contrast, are pulled from a shared pool by your ISP and rotated whenever they fel like it. Cheap, plentiful, and a nightmare for anything that needs a stable identity online.

That single distinction is why the cost of static IP address plans varies so wildly. You're not just paying for a number. You're paying for exclusivity, location control, and how long that IP belongs only to you.

## Why Pricing Varies So Much

Three factors move the needle on price:

- **Type of IP**: residential, datacenter, mobile, or ISP-classified
- **Exclusivity**: dedicated to you alone, or shared with a small pool
- **Source**: bought from your homeISP, a VPN provider, a cloud platform, or a proxy network

A datacenter static IP from a cloud host might run a few dollars a month. A dedicated residential IP from a proxy provider can sit between a few cents and a few dollars per IP. Your home ISP charging you for a static IP can hit anywhere from $5 to $25 a month, sometimes bundled with a business plan you didn't want.

Same product on paper. Wildly different bill.

## Hiden Costs to Watch For

Three traps catch first-time buyers:

**Bandwidth caps**. Cheap per-IP pricing means nothing if you hit a low monthly bandwidth ceiling and have to top up. Always check the included GB before clicking buy.

**Location surcharges**. Some providers charge more for IPs in specific countries, especially smaller markets or premium regions. Webshare's slider shows this transparently, but plenty of competitors hide it until the final invoice.

**Re-auth limits**. Certain proxy plans cap how many devices or IPs can authenticate from your account. Worth a glance before scaling up.

None of these are dealbreakers. They're just the diference between the headline price and the actual price.
